# Design Reference

Two reference sites guide this build. Use them as direction,
not as material to copy. Take structure, layout, and feel —
never copy their assets, copy, logos, or specific wording.
Everything is re-expressed in Refacint's brand (navy `#0E1435`
/ blue `#0D70DA`, Inter + DM Sans, light + dark).

## Reference 1 — Vysta (growwithvysta.com)

**Use for: the overall look and visual system.**

A premium paid-media agency site. What to take:

- **Bold, oversized display headlines.** Confident type
  hierarchy with large section titles and small uppercase
  eyebrows above them. Type does a lot of the design work.
- **Capability marquee.** A horizontally scrolling ticker of
  services/skills separated by bullets (e.g. "AI Agents •
  Workflow Automation • Custom CRMs • Custom Software •").
- **Large numeric proof blocks.** Big numbers paired with a
  short label, used as social proof.
- **Differentiator section ("Why clients chose us").** Three
  columns, each with a short label, a quote-style heading, a
  small decorative divider, and a paragraph.
- **Client logo wall.** A grid/row of client logos under a
  "brands you know" style heading.
- **Process / deliverable cards.** A row of icon cards, each
  describing a step or a thing the client gets.
- **Repeated, prominent CTA.** One primary action ("Book a
  call") appears multiple times down the page.
- **FAQ accordion.** Common questions answered plainly.
- **Polished multi-column footer** with link columns, contact,
  and trust badges.
- Clean, premium, lots of whitespace.

**What to AVOID from Vysta** (these break `brand-context.md`):

- Inflated or unverifiable metrics ("$1.5B generated",
  "$1M/day"). Afeez uses only real, verifiable numbers — or no
  number at all.
- Bravado and absolutist claims ("you'll NEVER need another
  agency", "no one else is doing this").
- Manufactured urgency and hard-sell CTAs.
- Tweet-flexing / screenshot social proof of money.
- Any "30 days to $X" framing.

Take Vysta's **polish and structure**, not its swagger.

## Reference 2 — Stanlee (stanlee.vercel.app)

**Use for: the content approach and how sections are written.**

A Lagos-based developer's portfolio. What to take:

- **Minimal navigation.** Three items: Projects, About, Contact.
- **First-person, honest hero.** A role eyebrow ("Fullstack
  Developer"), a value-prop headline, and a short, plain-spoken
  intro that states base (Lagos), reach (global clients), and
  specialization. No hype.
- **Story-driven project cards.** Each project is written as a
  short first-person story: the real problem, what was built,
  and the outcome — with links to the live site and the repo.
  This is the model for the Work section: specific, grounded,
  honest (Stanlee even notes a project built for a job he
  didn't get — that candor is the point).
- **"Tools of the craft" stack strip.** A simple labelled list
  of technologies/capabilities.
- **Personality-rich About.** First person, real voice, a bit
  of humor, plus credibility (worked across Nigeria and the
  US). Reads like a person, not a brochure.
- **Simple contact form** (name, email, message) with a warm
  one-line invitation.
- **Social links** in a "say hi" line.

Stanlee's honesty and first-person specificity are exactly the
voice in `brand-context.md`. This is the content benchmark.

## Stats Strip — Honesty Rule

Vysta's big-number blocks are visually effective, so the
**treatment** is worth borrowing — but the **numbers must be
real**. Afeez's true proof is modest and specific (e.g. named
clients like MOLEK Schools, Klassrun Technologies, Tenderville
School; years building; categories shipped). Acceptable stat
blocks use only numbers Afeez can stand behind. If a number
isn't real and verifiable, omit the stat rather than inflate
it. Never manufacture a metric to fill the layout. See
`brand-context.md`.

## Net Direction

A founder portfolio that **looks** as polished and confident
as a top agency site (Vysta), but **reads** as honest,
specific, and first-person as a real builder's portfolio
(Stanlee) — all in Refacint's navy/blue brand, light and dark.
